        For my good visualization, I chose the 338Canada Quebec popular vote projection (https://338canada.com/quebec/). I classified this as a good visualization because it is well suited to its audience, context, and data structure. Since this is a public-facing political visualization, the audience is likely made up of general readers who want to quickly understand where Quebec political parties stand. This matters because election data can easily become overwhelming if it is presented with too much technical detail. The horizontal bar chart fits the purpose well because the main goal is comparison: viewers can immediately see which parties are leading, which parties are close together, and which parties are further behind.
      
        This visualization also has strong perceptual quality. The message is easy to understand without much explanation. The use of party colours helps readers recognize each political party quickly, but the chart does not rely only on colour. Party names and projected percentages are labelled directly beside the bars, which reduces the need to move back and forth between the graph and a separate legend. This makes the visualization easier to read and more accessible. In this sense, the design choices reduce unnecessary cognitive load because the viewer can focus on the political pattern rather than figuring out how to read the chart.
      
        The visualization also has strong substantive quality because it does not present the projection as certain. The uncertainty ranges remind the viewer that election projections are based on polling and modelling, not guaranteed outcomes. This matters because political visualizations are not neutral objects; they can influence how people understand public life. Although no visualization is fully neutral, 338Canada’s design choices are rhetorically appropriate because they support the purpose of the graph: helping the public interpret a political projection quickly, clearly, and with some awareness of uncertainty. This connects to Midway’s (2020) argument that effective data visualizations should make patterns clear without misleading the audience. It also connects to Hehman and Xie’s (2021) emphasis on using visualizations to communicate patterns in data clearly. 

        I believe the “Total Revenue and Grant Income” chart (https://datavizproject.com/wp-content/uploads/examples/Sk%C3%A6rmbillede-2017-09-27-kl.-10.49.51.png, from the data viz bar graph section if the link does not work since it does not for me) is a bad visualization of the positive trend in revenue and grant income from 2012 until 2016 because the design obscures the ability to see the clear positive trend due to the way the chart is designed. The data behind the chart is simple, as evidenced by the fact that revenue and grant income increased from €27 million in 2012 to €85 million in 2016, in a linear manner. Therefore, the best method by which to demonstrate the rate of change would be using a simple line graph or a simple two-dimensional bar graph. However, the chart creates a three-dimensional decorative scene to illustrate an otherwise simple and straightforward linear time trend. 

        The major concern for me is with the addition of the unnecessary three-dimensional elements of this chart. The three-dimensional bar charts, perspective drawings, shading, and other dimensions make it more difficult for the viewer to accurately compare the values between the bars. In this case, instead of being able to compare the heights of the two-dimensional bars with the same reference axis, the viewer must also judge the angle and perspective at which the bars are displayed. This makes it much harder for the viewer to have an immediate understanding of the primary message of the chart – that the numbers are increasing. The viewer can understand that the numbers are increasing; however, the chart itself does not accomplish that strong of a message as clearly as it could have.

        The way this visual appears creates extra mental work for the viewer because the colourful background, the book layout, the shadows that surround the bars, and the patterned bars, when in combination with the data, create a competing distraction to the data being viewed. While these choices will create an aesthetically memorable page for the viewer, that aesthetic quality will not assist the viewer in understanding the trend depicted. Although this graphic possesses elements of aesthetic, substantive, and perceptual quality, the perceptual element of this visual is poor relative to how the decorative qualities of the graphic are distracting from the data; therefore, the graphic’s rhetorical function of helping the viewer interpret the information is weaker than the function of appearing to be impressive.

        The lack of a clearly defined y-axis and the lack of a visible scale are two reasons for the weak substantive quality of the graphic. Although the values are expressed above each bar and then repeated on the right-hand side of the graph, the graph provides insufficient structure to appropriately interpret the data represented. Additionally, the provenance rhetoric of the data is also weak because there is no clear way for the viewer to know how the data was collected. Midway (2020) states that good-quality visualizations should reduce ambiguity and facilitate easy comparisons; therefore, because this graphic emphasizes visual style over visual clarity, it violates the general principle outlined by Midway.

        Although the 338Canada visualization is effective overall, two improvements could strengthen it further. First, while a methodology note exists as a separate paragraph below the chart, integrating a brief source citation directly onto the visualization itself would improve its provenance rhetoric. As discussed in class, including a data source directly on a graphic signals transparency and trustworthiness to the viewer (Kennedy et al., 2016, as cited in course slides). Second, the chart relies heavily on party brand colours for differentiation. Since some of these colours may be difficult to distinguish for viewers with colour vision deficiencies, adopting a colour-blind accessible palette would improve the accessibility of the visualization for a broader audience (Midway, 2020).

        Two design changes would significantly improve the "Total Revenue and Grant Income" chart. First, and most importantly, the three-dimensional perspective elements should be replaced with a simple two-dimensional bar chart or line graph. As covered in class, 3D should not be used without a substantive reason, because perspective distortion makes it harder for viewers to accurately compare values and increases extraneous cognitive load (course slides, slide 44; Midway, 2020). The patterned and textured bar fills compound this problem further — as the Gestalt principle of similarity suggests, inconsistent visual treatments across bars imply meaningful differences between categories where none exist, misleading the viewer (course slides, slides 38–40). A flat 2D bar chart or line graph would allow viewers to immediately compare values against a shared reference axis, which is the most perceptually accurate encoding for this type of quantitative, sequential data showing change over time (Hehman & Xie, 2021).

        Second, the chart should include a clearly labelled y-axis with a consistent numeric scale, as well as a data source citation placed directly on the graphic. This would address the weak substantive quality of the chart by reducing ambiguity in how values are read and compared. It would also strengthen the chart's provenance rhetoric — the idea that citing a data source directly on a visualization signals transparency and increases viewer trust (Kennedy et al., 2016, as cited in course slides). Together, these two changes would bring the chart much closer to satisfying the aesthetic, substantive, and perceptual qualities of an effective data visualization.
